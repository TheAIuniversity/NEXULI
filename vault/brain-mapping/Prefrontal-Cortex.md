# Prefrontal Cortex (PFC)

## What It Processes
Executive function — decision-making, evaluation, planning, working memory, impulse control, goal-directed behavior.

Key sub-regions:
- **Dorsolateral PFC (dlPFC)**: Rational evaluation, comparison, working memory. "Is this worth my time?"
- **Ventromedial PFC (vmPFC)**: Value computation, reward prediction. "What's in it for me?"
- **Orbitofrontal Cortex (OFC)**: Expectation vs reality, outcome evaluation. "Did this match what I expected?"
- **Anterior Cingulate Cortex (ACC)**: Conflict monitoring, error detection. "Something doesn't add up here."

## TRIBE Vertex Range
Approximate fsaverage5 vertices: 5,121-8,192

## TRIBE Predictability
Pearson r: 0.1-0.2 (lower). Prefrontal responses are highly individual — same content triggers different evaluations in different people. But population-level patterns still emerge.

## Marketing Significance
- **High activation** = viewer actively evaluating content (engaged consideration)
- **Low activation** = passive consumption (entertainment mode, not buying mode)
- **dlPFC spike** = rational comparison triggered (feature list, pricing, competitor mention)
- **vmPFC spike** = value/reward signal (benefit statement, emotional payoff)
- **ACC spike** = something unexpected or contradictory (can be good: pattern interrupt, or bad: trust violation)

The prefrontal cortex is the **most important region for conversion**. High prefrontal activation during a CTA predicts action-taking. This is the "purchase intent" signal.

## Patterns Observed
- Questions spike PFC activation 40% more than statements
- "What if..." constructions are the strongest PFC activators
- Pricing information causes immediate dlPFC + ACC co-activation (evaluation + comparison)
- Social proof (testimonials, numbers) activates vmPFC (value validation)
- Urgency cues ("limited time") spike ACC (conflict: act now vs. wait)
- Too many choices cause PFC overload — activation drops paradoxically (choice paralysis)
- PFC activation naturally increases toward the end of content (anticipation of "what should I do?")
- Storytelling temporarily suppresses PFC (suspends evaluation) — dangerous if CTA follows immediately

## Optimization Rules
- If PFC low during CTA -> prime with question or comparison before CTA
- If PFC high throughout -> content is too "salesy" (constant evaluation = fatigue)
- If PFC spikes at wrong moments -> unintended evaluation triggers (remove or move them)
- If PFC and [[Default-Mode-Network]] both high -> rare "engaged emotion" state (optimal for conversion)
- Allow 3-5 seconds of non-evaluative content before CTA to "reset" PFC
- Then trigger PFC with question/comparison immediately before CTA

## PFC Activation Timeline for Conversion Content
```
0s          15s         30s         45s         60s
|-----------|-----------|-----------|-----------|
[Low PFC    ][Rising    ][Story/    ][PFC       ][CTA
 Hook/      ] PFC       ] Low PFC  ] prime     ] + PFC
 Emotion    ] Problem   ] Solution ] Question  ] peak
]           ] framing   ] delivery ]           ]
```

Ideal pattern: PFC starts low (emotional hook), rises during problem framing, dips during solution storytelling, then peaks sharply at the CTA moment.

## Decision Fatigue
PFC has limited "fuel." Extended evaluation depletes it:
- First 30 seconds: full capacity
- 30-60 seconds: 80% capacity
- 60-120 seconds: 60% capacity
- After 120 seconds: significant drop-off

Implication: Put the most important evaluation (CTA) early, or use emotional content (DMN activation) to "rest" PFC before the critical moment.
