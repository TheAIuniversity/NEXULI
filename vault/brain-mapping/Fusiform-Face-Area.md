# Fusiform Face Area (FFA)

## What It Processes
Face detection, face recognition, facial expression analysis, identity processing.

The FFA is a specialized region within the fusiform gyrus (ventral temporal cortex) that responds selectively to faces. It's one of the most robust findings in cognitive neuroscience — face processing is so important to humans that we evolved dedicated neural hardware for it.

Key functions:
- **Face detection**: Is there a face present? (fastest response, ~100ms)
- **Identity recognition**: Whose face is it? (slower, ~170ms)
- **Expression analysis**: What emotion are they showing? (feeds to amygdala and DMN)
- **Gaze direction**: Where are they looking? (critical for attention direction)

## TRIBE Vertex Range
Approximate fsaverage5 vertices: 11,265-12,288

## TRIBE Predictability
Pearson r: 0.4-0.6 (very good). FFA responses are highly predictable because face presence is a concrete, detectable stimulus feature. This is one of the key validations of TRIBE — it correctly identifies face-responsive regions without being told about them.

## Marketing Significance
- **High activation** = face(s) prominently visible and engaging
- **Low activation** = no faces, or faces too small/obscured
- **Spike** = new face appearing, especially with direct eye contact
- **Sustained high** = face-forward content (talking head, interview)

Faces are the single most powerful visual element for capturing and holding attention. The FFA acts as a "face magnet" — when a face is present, it pulls visual processing resources toward it, often at the expense of other visual elements.

## Patterns Observed
- Face in first 2 seconds -> +40% fusiform activation vs. no-face opening
- Direct eye contact produces 25% higher FFA activation than profile view
- Emotional expressions > neutral faces (fear/surprise highest, then joy, then anger)
- Close-up face > medium shot > wide shot (activation scales inversely with face size in frame)
- Multiple faces activate FFA but at lower per-face intensity (divided processing)
- Animated/cartoon faces activate FFA at ~70% of real face levels
- Text overlaid on a face reduces FFA activation (visual competition)
- Face partially obscured (mask, sunglasses) produces moderate activation + ACC spike (uncertainty)

## Optimization Rules
- For thumbnails: include a face with emotional expression for maximum FFA activation
- For hooks: face in frame 1 with direct eye contact is the strongest pattern
- If FFA low in talking-head content -> camera too far, move closer
- If FFA high but [[Prefrontal-Cortex]] low -> face is engaging but message isn't landing
- If FFA drops during key message -> face disappeared or became too small
- Don't overlay text on the face region — put text adjacent to face
- For B-roll: include human faces periodically to maintain engagement anchor

## Face + Emotion Chain
When FFA detects a face:
1. FFA activates (face detected) -> 100ms
2. Amygdala activates (emotion read) -> 150ms
3. [[Default-Mode-Network]] activates (social cognition: empathy, theory of mind) -> 300ms
4. [[Prefrontal-Cortex]] may activate (evaluation: trust, credibility) -> 500ms

This chain means a face triggers a cascade of social processing. A trustworthy, emotional face can activate the entire brain in under a second.

## Common Mistakes
- **Face too small**: If face occupies <15% of frame area, FFA activation drops significantly
- **Multiple faces competing**: More than 3 faces causes "FFA overload" — none processed deeply
- **Face at wrong moment**: Face during data slide pulls attention away from data
- **Fake/stock faces**: Viewers' FFA + ACC co-activate (face detected + "something off") — uncanny valley effect reduces trust
