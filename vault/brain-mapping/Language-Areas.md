# Language Areas (Broca's + Wernicke's + Angular Gyrus)

## What It Processes
Language comprehension and production — syntax, semantics, narrative structure, word meaning.

Three key sub-regions:
- **Broca's Area (left inferior frontal gyrus)**: Syntax processing, sentence structure, grammatical relationships. Also involved in speech production planning.
- **Wernicke's Area (left posterior superior temporal gyrus)**: Semantic processing, word meaning, vocabulary access. Bridges auditory input to conceptual understanding.
- **Angular Gyrus (left inferior parietal)**: Cross-modal integration, metaphor understanding, abstract concept mapping. Where language meets spatial and visual concepts.

## TRIBE Vertex Range
Approximate fsaverage5 vertices: 3,585-5,120

## TRIBE Predictability
Pearson r: 0.3-0.5 (good). LLaMA 3.2 features predict language area responses well, especially for narrative content.

## Marketing Significance
- **High activation** = message is being processed and understood
- **Low activation** = words not landing, too simple, or too complex
- **Broca's spike** = complex sentence encountered (can be good or bad)
- **Wernicke's spike** = new/surprising word or concept encountered
- **Angular gyrus spike** = metaphor or analogy processed successfully

Language areas tell you whether your message is actually being cognitively processed, not just heard. High auditory + low language = sound is pleasant but meaning is not registering.

## Patterns Observed
- Simple, direct sentences activate language areas efficiently (quick spike, quick resolve)
- Complex jargon causes sustained Broca's activation (processing struggle)
- Metaphors activate angular gyrus strongly — "Your ROI will skyrocket" > "Your ROI will increase"
- Stories activate language areas more broadly than facts (narrative engages Wernicke's + angular gyrus)
- Repetition of key phrases creates reinforcement patterns (each repetition slightly lower activation but better retention)
- Questions activate language areas + [[Prefrontal-Cortex]] together (evaluation mode)
- Lists are processed differently than prose — faster Broca's activation, less Wernicke's
- Concrete words > abstract words for activation strength

## Optimization Rules
- If language activation is low despite speech -> simplify vocabulary or slow down delivery
- If Broca's activation is sustained (not spiking) -> sentences too complex, simplify syntax
- If Wernicke's drops during key value proposition -> word choice not resonating, try different vocabulary
- If language areas and [[Prefrontal-Cortex]] co-activate -> message is being evaluated (good for CTA)
- Target language activation within 2s of key message delivery
- Use metaphors strategically to spike angular gyrus at memorable moments

## Message Complexity Sweet Spot
| Audience | Optimal Complexity | Broca's Pattern |
|----------|-------------------|----------------|
| General consumer | Grade 6-8 reading level | Quick spikes, fast resolution |
| Professional/B2B | Grade 10-12 | Moderate sustained, periodic spikes |
| Technical/Expert | Grade 14+ | Higher sustained, deep processing |

The key is matching complexity to audience. Overly simple content for experts causes disengagement (language areas go flat). Overly complex content for general audiences causes processing failure (Broca's saturates, comprehension drops).

## Copywriting Implications
- **Headlines**: Spike Wernicke's with unexpected word combinations
- **Body copy**: Alternate simple and complex sentences (activation variance)
- **CTAs**: Simple syntax (low Broca's load) + strong verb (Wernicke's spike)
- **Storytelling**: Sustained moderate activation across all three sub-regions
- **Data/Stats**: Brief Broca's spike followed by angular gyrus (number-to-concept mapping)
